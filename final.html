UN General Debate · 1970 – 2015

How the World Speaks

Once a year, every country in the United Nations gives a speech to the General Assembly. Between 1970 and 2015 that produced 7,507 speeches — about 20 million words. This page walks through what was said, how it changed over time, and lets you look at the data country by country.

Chapter 1 · What is this data?

What is the General Debate?

Once a year, in late September, the United Nations holds a week-long event called the General Debate. The rules are simple: each member country sends one person (usually a president, a prime minister, or a foreign minister) to give a speech in front of the General Assembly. There is no actual debate in the usual sense. Each country gets its turn, says what it wants to say, and sits back down.

Since 1970 that adds up to about 7,500 speeches. They cover wars, climate, trade, human rights, development, and pretty much every issue you can think of. They are public records, but very few people actually read them. Each speech is around 3,000 words on average, and they pile up fast.

This page goes through that pile in five short chapters. We start with the basic numbers: how many speeches there are, who gave them, and how long they are. Then we look at the words themselves: which ones come up most often, and how the topics have changed since the 1970s. At the end, you can open the world map and look at the data for any country.

Chapter 2 · How many speeches

How many speeches, and from how many countries?

From 1970 to 2015 the General Debate collected 7,507 speeches among 199 countries. The number of speeches grew steadily over time. In 1970, only about 70 countries showed up. By 2015 nearly 200 did.

Two periods explain most of the growth. The 1970s saw a wave of new arrivals as former colonies in Africa, Asia and the Caribbean became independent. The early 1990s saw another jump, when the Soviet Union split into 15 new states and Yugoslavia into several more. After that, growth slowed down. The chart below shows one bar per year.

Chapter 3 · The most common words

What words come up the most?

If you take out short common words like the, and, is, and count what's left, the same handful of words show up over and over again: peace, development, security, economic, human, rights. Every country uses them. At first glance, every speech sounds about the same.

That's part of the problem with reading UN speeches in bulk: the big themes are universal. The real differences only show up when you ask harder questions, for example which kind of peace, whose security, about which countries. We'll come back to that in later chapters. Chapter 4 · How topics changed

What changed over the years?

Speeches don't talk about the same things forever. Some topics fade out, others arrive suddenly, and a few stay on the agenda the whole time. A handful of keywords is enough to see the pattern.

During the 1970s and 1980s, nuclear weapons came up constantly. The Cold War made disarmament a permanent topic. After the Soviet Union dissolved in 1991, nuclear mentions dropped sharply, and democracy rose in its place. After the September 11 attacks in 2001, terror jumped from almost nothing to one of the most-mentioned words for years. Climate only became a regular topic after 2007, around the time the UN started running yearly climate summits.

The white dots along the bottom mark key geopolitical events — hover one to see what it was. The numbers on the y-axis are mentions per 1,000 words, not raw counts, so a country that wrote a short focused speech about climate scores higher here than a country that wrote a long speech about everything.

What we noticed in the numbers

12001 · Terror

Pre-2001, "terror" averaged 0.39 mentions per 1,000 words across every year of speeches. In 2001 — held days after the September attacks — it hit 6.31. That's 16× the entire pre-9/11 average. The next-biggest spike before then was 1998 (US embassy bombings in Africa) at just 1.39.

21996 · Nuclear's real peak

You'd expect the Cold War to be peak nuclear, but the all-time high is actually 1996 at 2.02 — the year the UN finalised the Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty. The Cold War kept nuclear constantly loud (1.0–1.7 every year from 1975 to 1986), but the single biggest year came after the wall fell.

32007 · Climate arrived in one year

Before 2007, climate averaged 0.20 mentions per 1,000 words — a footnote. In 2007 it jumped 12× to 2.34, the year of the IPCC's Fourth Assessment Report and the Bali roadmap. It peaked again in 2009 (Copenhagen prep) at 2.40, and has never fallen back to its pre-2007 baseline. Climate became a permanent agenda item, not a fad.

4Topics trade places

In 2001, with terror at 6.31, nuclear fell to its lowest point in the dataset (0.39). When one global emergency took the stage, the previous one nearly vanished. We see the same pattern when the financial crisis arrives: economic mentions jump from 1.82 in 2008 to 3.05 in 2009 while almost every other topic dips.

51991 · Democracy's loudest year

Democracy hit 1.10 mentions per 1,000 words in 1991 — its all-time peak. The new states that had just emerged from the Soviet bloc spent their first UN speeches asserting their democratic credentials. The rate has slowly drifted downward ever since.

62015 · Refugee crisis

Refugee mentions sat at an average of 0.24 per 1,000 words across 1990–2014. In 2015, with over a million Syrians and others arriving in Europe, it leapt to 0.77 — three times its long-term average and the highest of any year in the dataset.

Chapter 5 · The topic map

Where in the world is each topic talked about?

The global trends in the previous chapter hide a lot. When the chart says "climate is rising", it doesn't tell you whose climate — whether small island states are screaming about rising seas while big polluters stay quiet, or the other way around. To see that, you need to put the topic on a map.

Pick a topic from the dropdown. Drag the year slider, or hit play to scrub through all 46 years automatically. Each country is colored by how often it mentioned the topic's keywords in that year's speech, normalised per 1,000 words. Grey countries didn't speak that year. Hover for the exact rate.

The color scale is fixed per topic — so the deepest red always means the highest rate that topic ever reached, across all years. That lets you compare years honestly: a country lighting up in 2009 really did mention climate more than the same country in 1990.

Hover the brightest reds: Turkey (22.97), Saudi Arabia (22.28), Russia (22.09). 2Climate · 2009

The brightest spots are not the big polluters. They are Tuvalu (15.4), Kiribati (14.0), Tonga (13.3), Seychelles (15.5), plus Guyana, Suriname, Cambodia. The Pacific and Indian Ocean small island states — the ones literally going underwater — took the loudest stand at Copenhagen.

3Migration · 2014 → 2015

Slide one year. In 1980 the top voices on migration were Australia, Papua New Guinea, Somalia and Sudan — countries shaped by post-colonial displacement. In 2015, with the Syrian war driving over a million people into Europe, the top eight are all European: Hungary (17.5), the EU itself (13.6), Austria, Switzerland, Luxembourg, France, Malta, Estonia. Migration flipped from a Southern issue to a European one in 35 years.

4Cold War vocabulary · slide 1975 → 1995

Watch entire blocs fade. Cuba sits at 6.8 in 1975 (imperialism, capitalism, soviet) and drops to 0.0 by 1995. East Germany is at 5.7 in 1975 then simply disappears (no more speeches after 1989). The US runs the opposite way: 1.7 in 1975, peaking at 6.9 in 1985 at the height of Reagan-era rhetoric, then back down to 0.89 by 1995. By the year 2000, almost nobody in the Assembly is using these words.

Chapter 6 · Diplomatic blocs

Who talks like whom?

Topics tell us what was said. Blocs ask a harder question — who said it the same way. We took every country's speeches in each decade, treated them as one big document, and used a standard text-similarity technique (TF-IDF, then t-SNE) to put each country on a 2D map.

Two dots near each other means those two countries used a similar vocabulary that decade. Two dots far apart means they spoke about different things, or used different words about the same things. The axes themselves don't mean anything specific — only distances do.

Pick a decade. Hit play to watch the map shuffle as the world's diplomatic vocabulary shifts. Hover any dot for the country and decade; click to read one of its actual speeches.

What the map shows

1USA ↔ Cuba

In the 1970s, the two were already on opposite sides, but the gap was modest (distance 0.60 on the normalised map). It widened steadily: 0.72 in the 80s, 0.83 in the 90s, 1.45 in the 2000s. By the 2010s the two countries are talking about almost completely different worlds.

2France ↔ United Kingdom

For three decades — 1970s, 80s, 90s — France and the UK are practically on top of each other on the map (distance ≈ 0.08). They were the prototypical aligned Western powers. From the 2000s onward the gap jumps almost ten-fold to 0.72; the war on terror and Iraq split their diplomatic language for the first time.

3African Group

The green dots form one of the tightest clusters in every decade. Despite huge differences in size and politics, the post-independence African states have spoken in a recognisably common voice at the UN — focused on development, decolonisation and sovereignty — for the entire 46-year window.

4The 1990s shake-up

Compare 1980s and 1990s. The Eastern bloc — present in the 80s — fragments visibly in the 90s as new states emerge (their first speeches sound more like Western Europe than like the old USSR). The biggest spatial reshuffle in the whole series happens at this transition.

Chapter 7 · Who talks about whom

Whose name comes up in whose speeches?

Every speech is full of country names. We counted them. For each country, we know how many times it named every other country across the 46 years — 160,662 mentions in total, threaded across the entire UN General Debate.

    1Asymmetric obsession · Cuba ↔ USA

    Across all 46 years, Cuba mentions the United States 662 times. The United States mentions Cuba only 10 times. Cuba's diplomatic identity at the UN is built around opposing the US; from Washington, Cuba is a footnote.

    2Israel's top talking point

    Israel's most-mentioned country across the whole dataset isn't the United States or any neighbour — it's Iran (262 mentions), more than its mentions of Egypt (173), Lebanon (164), Syria (129) and Jordan (115) combined. The Iranian threat dominates Israeli diplomacy at the UN.

    3The most-talked-about country

    Counting incoming mentions, the USA (11,331) is unsurprisingly first. But South Africa is a close second (10,661), ahead of Israel (10,527). For three decades the world's speeches were dominated by apartheid; once Mandela was free, the curve fell hard. Namibia (7,387) is fourth — same apartheid story.

    4Russia ↔ USA

    Russia's all-time top mention is the USA (358). The USA's all-time top mention is Russia (199). The Cold War rivalry left a permanent fingerprint on each side's UN vocabulary — even decades after 1991.

    Chapter 8 · Distinctive vocabulary

    Every country has its own dictionary

    Globally, every country says peace and development. But every country also has a private dictionary — words it reaches for far more than the rest of the world. France talks about francophonie. Cuba talks about Yankee, Fidel and blockade. Tuvalu talks about atolls and SIDS. The Holy See talks about the Pope.

    We measured this by comparing each word's frequency inside a country's speeches to its frequency in the global corpus. A ratio of 10 means the word is ten times more common in that country's mouth than in the world's. We keep words that score at least 2×, that appear at least 5 times in the country and 50 times globally — enough to filter OCR noise while keeping country-specific vocabulary intact.

    What the words reveal

    1Cuba's revolutionary vocabulary

    Cuba's top words are yankee (93× more common than average), fidel (72×), castro (70×), cubans (67×), CIA (52×), Puerto Rico (42×), Allende (43×), Martí (36×). Castro-era anti-imperialist rhetoric is mathematically detectable from speech alone.

    2Tuvalu's survival agenda

    Tuvalu's vocabulary reads like a survival checklist: tuvalu (474× — they have to name themselves a lot), LDC (least developed country, 211×), graduation (LDC graduation status, 108×), SDGs, SIDS, atoll, insurance, remittances, emissions, Kyoto. Almost every word ties back to climate and small-state status.

    3Israel vs Palestine — same conflict, different words

    Israel's distinctive words: hamas, jewish, jews, anti-semitism, rockets, bible, incitement. Palestine's: rabin, siege, redeployment, demolition, strip (Gaza), settlers, aqsa, arafat, mosques. The two countries describe the same physical conflict using almost entirely non-overlapping vocabulary — a vivid example of how language shapes politics.

    4North Korea's leader cult

    Kim Jong (307×), Kim (205×), Sung (149× — for Kim il-Sung), Pyongyang (69×), denuclearization, armistice, peninsula, reunified. The DPRK's UN vocabulary is overwhelmingly about its leadership and the unresolved Korean War.

    5The Holy See is unmistakable

    The Vatican's top words: XVI (Pope Benedict, 164×), Francis (Pope Francis, 119×), pope (112×), holy, believers, catholic, holiness, church, christians. The smallest UN state has the most linguistically distinct fingerprint in the entire dataset — there is genuinely no other country talking like this.
