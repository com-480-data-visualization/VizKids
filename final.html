How the World Speaks

Chapter 1 · What is this data?

What is the General Debate?

Once a year, in late September, the United Nations holds a week-long event called the General Debate. The rules are simple: each member country sends one person (usually a president, a prime minister, or a foreign minister) to give a speech in front of the General Assembly. There is no actual debate in the usual sense. Each country gets its turn, says what it wants to say, and sits back down.

Since 1970 that adds up to about 7,500 speeches. They cover wars, climate, trade, human rights, development, and pretty much every issue you can think of. They are public records, but very few people actually read them. Each speech is around 3,000 words on average, and they pile up fast.

This page goes through that pile in five short chapters. We start with the basic numbers: how many speeches there are, who gave them, and how long they are. Then we look at the words themselves: which ones come up most often, and how the topics have changed since the 1970s. At the end, you can open the world map and look at the data for any country.

Chapter 2 · How many speeches

How many speeches, and from how many countries?

From 1970 to 2015 the General Debate collected 7,507 speeches among 199 countries. The number of speeches grew steadily over time. In 1970, only about 70 countries showed up. By 2015 nearly 200 did.

Two periods explain most of the growth. The 1970s saw a wave of new arrivals as former colonies in Africa, Asia and the Caribbean became independent. The early 1990s saw another jump, when the Soviet Union split into 15 new states and Yugoslavia into several more. After that, growth slowed down. The chart below shows one bar per year.

Chapter 3 · The most common words

What words come up the most?

If you take out short common words like the, and, is, and count what's left, the same handful of words show up over and over again: peace, development, security, economic, human, rights. Every country uses them. At first glance, every speech sounds about the same.

That's part of the problem with reading UN speeches in bulk: the big themes are universal. The real differences only show up when you ask harder questions, for example which kind of peace, whose security, about which countries. We'll come back to that in later chapters. Chapter 4 · How topics changed

What changed over the years?

Speeches don't talk about the same things forever. Some topics fade out, others arrive suddenly, and a few stay on the agenda the whole time. A handful of keywords is enough to see the pattern.

During the 1970s and 1980s, nuclear weapons came up constantly. The Cold War made disarmament a permanent topic. After the Soviet Union dissolved in 1991, nuclear mentions dropped sharply, and democracy rose in its place. After the September 11 attacks in 2001, terror jumped from almost nothing to one of the most-mentioned words for years. Climate only became a regular topic after 2007, around the time the UN started running yearly climate summits.

The white dots along the bottom mark key geopolitical events — hover one to see what it was. The numbers on the y-axis are mentions per 1,000 words, not raw counts, so a country that wrote a short focused speech about climate scores higher here than a country that wrote a long speech about everything.

What we noticed in the numbers

12001 · Terror

Pre-2001, "terror" averaged 0.39 mentions per 1,000 words across every year of speeches. In 2001 — held days after the September attacks — it hit 6.31. That's 16× the entire pre-9/11 average. The next-biggest spike before then was 1998 (US embassy bombings in Africa) at just 1.39.

21996 · Nuclear's real peak

You'd expect the Cold War to be peak nuclear, but the all-time high is actually 1996 at 2.02 — the year the UN finalised the Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty. The Cold War kept nuclear constantly loud (1.0–1.7 every year from 1975 to 1986), but the single biggest year came after the wall fell.

32007 · Climate arrived in one year

Before 2007, climate averaged 0.20 mentions per 1,000 words — a footnote. In 2007 it jumped 12× to 2.34, the year of the IPCC's Fourth Assessment Report and the Bali roadmap. It peaked again in 2009 (Copenhagen prep) at 2.40, and has never fallen back to its pre-2007 baseline. Climate became a permanent agenda item, not a fad.

4Topics trade places

In 2001, with terror at 6.31, nuclear fell to its lowest point in the dataset (0.39). When one global emergency took the stage, the previous one nearly vanished. We see the same pattern when the financial crisis arrives: economic mentions jump from 1.82 in 2008 to 3.05 in 2009 while almost every other topic dips.

51991 · Democracy's loudest year

Democracy hit 1.10 mentions per 1,000 words in 1991 — its all-time peak. The new states that had just emerged from the Soviet bloc spent their first UN speeches asserting their democratic credentials. The rate has slowly drifted downward ever since.

62015 · Refugee crisis

Refugee mentions sat at an average of 0.24 per 1,000 words across 1990–2014. In 2015, with over a million Syrians and others arriving in Europe, it leapt to 0.77 — three times its long-term average and the highest of any year in the dataset.

Chapter 5 · The topic map

Where in the world is each topic talked about?

The global trends in the previous chapter hide a lot. When the chart says "climate is rising", it doesn't tell you whose climate — whether small island states are screaming about rising seas while big polluters stay quiet, or the other way around. To see that, you need to put the topic on a map.

Pick a topic from the dropdown. Drag the year slider, or hit play to scrub through all 46 years automatically. Each country is colored by how often it mentioned the topic's keywords in that year's speech, normalised per 1,000 words. Grey countries didn't speak that year. Hover for the exact rate.

The color scale is fixed per topic — so the deepest red always means the highest rate that topic ever reached, across all years. That lets you compare years honestly: a country lighting up in 2009 really did mention climate more than the same country in 1990.
